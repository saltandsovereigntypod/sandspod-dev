How Hekate Inspired A Spiritual Home for Modern Witchcraft

July 2026

✦ ☽ ✦ ☾ ✦

"I thought I was building a website."

Today’s episode is unlike anything I’ve recorded before because, for the first time, I get to share a project that has completely taken over my life for the last three weeks. I don’t mean that figuratively, either. For three straight weeks, nearly every moment outside of my full-time job has been spent sitting in front of my computer, building something that, somewhere along the way, became so much bigger than I ever expected it to be. Until now, I haven’t mentioned it on the podcast because I wasn’t entirely sure how to explain what I was creating. The more I worked on it, the more it evolved, and every time I thought I finally understood what it was becoming, another piece would fall into place and change my perspective all over again. It wasn’t until a few days ago that I finally found the words for it.

I thought I was building a website.

That sentence is technically true, but it also feels incredibly incomplete. If I only told you that I’d spent the last three weeks building a website, you’d probably picture something fairly ordinary. Maybe you’re imagining a place to listen to podcast episodes, read a few blog posts, browse upcoming books, or learn a little more about who I am. To be fair, that’s exactly what I imagined too. In fact, when I first purchased the domain SaltandSovereignty.com, that was the entire vision. I wanted a home for the podcast, a place where everything could live together, and maybe, years from now, somewhere I could eventually host retreats or share other projects. It was a simple idea, one that felt practical and manageable, and if you’d asked me then what I hoped it would become, I probably could have explained the whole thing in less than a minute.

Looking back now, it’s almost funny how small that vision was. Not because there was anything wrong with it, but because I had absolutely no idea where it would eventually lead. If someone had told me that this little website would grow into a digital altar, a Book of Shadows, a Living Library, an Apothecary, and eventually a place where people from all over the world could build and document their own spiritual practices, I would have laughed and assumed they had mistaken me for somebody else. None of those ideas existed when I bought that domain. They weren’t sitting in a notebook waiting for the right opportunity, and they certainly weren’t part of some elaborate master plan. They simply didn’t exist yet. Somehow, over the course of three weeks, one idea kept leading naturally into another until I looked up from my computer one evening and realized I wasn’t solving isolated problems anymore. I was building pieces of something much larger, even if I couldn’t fully explain what that something was.

The strange part is that I don’t think this story actually begins three weeks ago. That’s when I started writing the code, but the foundation had been quietly forming long before I ever opened my laptop. In hindsight, I can trace almost every decision I made back to the conversations we’ve been having here on this podcast over the last year. Every episode about healing, every conversation about sovereignty, every reflection on spirituality, every story I’ve shared about my own relationship with Hekate had been shaping the way I viewed this project without me even realizing it. At the time, they all felt like separate pieces of my life. It wasn’t until recently that I understood they had been pointing toward the same destination all along.

Many of you know that my spiritual path has become deeply intertwined with Hekate, but there’s one story I’ve never shared in its entirety because, honestly, I wasn’t sure I was ready to. There was a period not that long ago when I seriously considered walking away from this podcast. It wasn’t because I had stopped loving it or because I regretted starting it. Quite the opposite, actually. I cared so deeply about what I was creating that it became painful to wonder whether it was making any difference at all. Every episode required hours of research, writing, recording, editing, and publishing, and despite how much I enjoyed the process, I couldn’t shake the feeling that maybe I was speaking into an empty room. The audience was still small, growth was slow, and I found myself wondering if anyone would even notice if I quietly stopped pressing record each week.

Eventually, I brought those questions into meditation because I didn’t know what else to do with them. I wasn’t asking for a miracle or expecting some dramatic spiritual experience. I simply wanted clarity. I wanted to know whether it was time to let the podcast go or whether I needed to keep showing up, even if I couldn’t yet see where it was leading. During that meditation, I experienced something that has stayed with me ever since. I heard Hekate say, “You are to be my voice.” Before I go any further, I want to be very clear about something. I’m not sharing that because I expect you to believe my experience or because I think it somehow places me in a position of authority. It doesn’t. I’m simply telling you what happened. Whether someone listening believes that was Hekate, my subconscious, or something else entirely is a question everyone gets to answer for themselves. All I know is that, in that moment, those words felt more real than anything I had experienced before.

When the meditation ended, my circumstances hadn’t changed at all. The podcast wasn’t suddenly reaching thousands of people, I wasn’t making money from it, and I certainly wasn’t sitting on some brilliant idea for a platform that would one day consume every waking hour of three straight weeks. The only thing that had changed was my willingness to keep going. So I recorded another episode, and then another after that. I stopped measuring success by how quickly the audience was growing and started focusing on whether I was creating something that felt honest. Looking back now, I realize I wasn’t just choosing to continue a podcast. Without knowing it, I was laying the first stones of a path that would eventually lead me to the project I’ve spent the last three weeks building. At the time, I thought those two stories were completely separate. Now I can’t imagine telling one without the other.

At some point, without really noticing when it happened, I stopped paying attention to download numbers the way I had in the beginning. That’s not because the audience suddenly exploded overnight. It didn’t. Growth was slow, and honestly, it still is. But somewhere along the way, I realized I had been measuring success by the wrong thing. I had been looking at numbers on a screen when I should have been paying attention to the people behind them. There’s a profound difference between those two perspectives, and once I saw it, I don’t think I could ever look at this podcast the same way again.

Most episodes are listened to by around twenty people. Now, in a world where everything seems to revolve around algorithms, follower counts, and going viral, twenty probably doesn’t sound very impressive. Social media has a way of convincing us that if thousands of people aren’t watching, then what we’re creating must not be worth much. I think a lot of us have quietly absorbed that message, whether we realize it or not. Bigger is better. Faster is better. More is always better. It’s a mindset that’s so deeply woven into our culture that we rarely stop to question it.

Then one morning, I found myself thinking about those twenty listeners in a completely different way. Instead of picturing a number on an analytics dashboard, I imagined twenty people sitting together in my living room every Thursday morning. Twenty chairs filled with twenty human beings, each carrying their own joys, fears, losses, hopes, and questions about life. Twenty people who had intentionally chosen to spend part of their morning with me when they could have been doing countless other things instead. Suddenly, twenty didn’t feel small anymore. It felt incredibly personal. It felt like a privilege.

That shift changed something in me. I stopped asking myself how quickly this community was growing and started asking a much more meaningful question. If someone chose to spend time with me every week, what kind of space was I creating for them? Were they leaving feeling more afraid, or more empowered? More dependent on my opinions, or more confident in their own discernment? Was I encouraging people to follow me, or was I encouraging them to trust themselves? Those questions mattered infinitely more than whether the next episode performed a little better than the last one.

The more I reflected on those questions, the more my thoughts began drifting beyond the listeners who had already found the podcast. I started thinking about the people who hadn’t discovered it yet, and the lives they might be living. I thought about the teenager who kept their interest in witchcraft hidden because they knew their parents wouldn’t understand. I thought about the college student whose entire spiritual practice had to fit inside a single drawer because they shared a dorm room with someone else. I thought about the person living with chronic illness who desperately wanted to spend an afternoon tending an altar but barely had enough energy to make dinner. I thought about the single parent carefully choosing between buying groceries and buying the ritual supplies they’d been wanting for months. None of these people were real individuals I knew personally, but they represented situations I knew existed, and once those possibilities entered my mind, I couldn’t stop thinking about them.

It also forced me to look honestly at my own journey. So much of my relationship with Hekate had been shaped by opportunities I hadn’t even recognized as opportunities because they were simply part of my everyday life. I had privacy when I wanted to meditate. I had shelves where I could keep books without worrying someone would throw them away. I had a home where I could leave an altar standing without wondering who might discover it, and over time I had been able to slowly collect tools that became meaningful to my practice. None of those things had ever felt extraordinary. They were simply the backdrop against which my spirituality had unfolded. It wasn’t until I imagined people whose circumstances looked very different from mine that I realized how much access I had quietly taken for granted.

We often talk about privilege through the lenses of money, education, healthcare, or opportunity, and those conversations are incredibly important. But I don’t think we spend nearly enough time acknowledging that spiritual access can also be a privilege. Being able to light a candle without fear is a privilege. Having a private journal that no one else will read is a privilege. Being physically healthy enough to gather herbs, clean an altar, or stand comfortably through a ritual is a privilege. Even something as simple as having a safe place to ask questions without worrying how people around you will respond is something many of us don’t think twice about until we imagine life without it. I don’t say any of this to make anyone feel guilty for what they have. I say it because recognizing our own privileges changes the questions we’re willing to ask.

The question that kept returning to me was surprisingly simple, yet I couldn’t seem to shake it. If spirituality had become one of the greatest sources of healing, growth, and meaning in my own life, why should someone else’s access to those same possibilities depend on circumstances they never chose? Why should the ability to build a relationship with the divine be limited by money, physical health, living arrangements, or geography? The more I sat with those questions, the more impossible they became to ignore. They followed me while I was driving to work, while I was making dinner, and eventually while I was sitting in front of my computer building what I still believed was just a podcast website.

Without realizing it, every decision I made after that started passing through a filter I hadn’t consciously created. It wasn’t a question about design or technology, and it certainly wasn’t about creating the most impressive collection of features. Instead, every new idea had to answer one simple question before it earned a place in the project: Does this make spiritual practice more accessible? If the answer was yes, I kept building. If the answer was no, it didn’t matter how interesting the feature seemed because it wasn’t serving the purpose that was quietly taking shape beneath everything else. Looking back now, I think that single question changed the trajectory of the entire project. At the time, though, I still hadn’t realized just how dramatically it was about to redefine what I was building.

There was just one problem.

Once I started asking myself whether each new idea made spiritual practice more accessible, the original plan for the website began falling apart. The more honestly I answered that question, the more obvious it became that a simple podcast website was never going to accomplish what I wanted it to. A place to listen to episodes was helpful, but it didn’t remove barriers. A blog was valuable, but it didn’t create access. Every solution I came up with seemed to expose another problem that hadn’t existed five minutes earlier, and before long I found myself chasing questions I hadn’t intended to ask when I first opened my computer three weeks before.

I never sat down one morning and decided I was going to build a digital altar. In fact, if someone had suggested the idea to me a month ago, I probably would have smiled politely and changed the subject. It sounded strange, almost contradictory. Spirituality has always been something I associate with tangible experiences, lighting a candle, holding herbs in my hands, feeling the warmth of a flame or the texture of stone beneath my fingertips. None of that seemed like something technology could meaningfully contribute to. But the more I thought about the people I’d been imagining, the more I realized I wasn’t trying to recreate a physical altar. I was trying to solve a problem.

What about the person who couldn’t have an altar at all?

That question refused to leave me alone. I kept imagining someone living in circumstances where displaying spiritual tools simply wasn’t an option. Maybe they lived with family who wouldn’t understand. Maybe they shared a bedroom with roommates. Maybe they were navigating an abusive relationship, living in temporary housing, or recovering from an illness that made gathering physical supplies impossible. Whatever the reason, the result was the same. They weren’t choosing not to practice. Their circumstances were choosing for them. Once I looked at it through that lens, the idea of a digital altar no longer felt like replacing something sacred. It felt like creating an opportunity where none had existed before.

Almost immediately, another question surfaced. If someone could perform a ritual, where would they record what happened afterward? Every meaningful spiritual practice eventually creates stories worth remembering. There’s the meditation that unexpectedly changes your perspective, the dream that lingers in your mind for weeks, the herb that begins carrying personal significance beyond anything you’ve read in a book, or the ritual that quietly becomes a turning point in your life. Those moments deserve somewhere to live. That’s where the idea of a digital Book of Shadows began to emerge, not because I thought journaling needed to become digital, but because I wanted someone’s practice to remain with them wherever life happened to take them.

As I continued building, I noticed something fascinating beginning to happen. Every new feature naturally pointed toward another one. If someone was writing about rosemary in their journal, wouldn’t it be helpful if they could immediately pull up traditional correspondences without opening three different books? If those correspondences were available, wouldn’t it also make sense to give people a place to record what rosemary had meant in their own practice, especially if their experience differed from what tradition suggested? Before long, one idea was leading effortlessly into the next. It didn’t feel like I was inventing something anymore. It felt like I was uncovering connections that had been there all along.

One realization, in particular, completely changed the direction of the project. I began thinking about how often modern spirituality asks us to choose between tradition and personal experience, as though those two things exist in opposition to one another. On one side, there are historical correspondences, centuries of folklore, and practices handed down through generations. On the other, there’s your own lived experience, the relationship you’ve built with the divine through years of trial, reflection, and personal discovery. I’ve never believed those two things have to compete. Tradition gives us roots, but lived experience allows those roots to continue growing. One connects us to the people who came before us, while the other reminds us that spirituality is still alive today. Both have something valuable to offer, and I found myself wanting to create a space where they could exist side by side without either one diminishing the other.

That realization eventually became what I now call the Living Library. Rather than blending everything together into one overwhelming collection of information, I wanted to intentionally separate three different voices. There would be traditional knowledge, preserving the wisdom that has shaped spiritual practice for generations. There would be your own observations, because no book can tell you what a particular herb, crystal, deity, or ritual has meant in your life. And eventually, there would be a place for community knowledge, not as unquestionable truth, but simply as people generously sharing what had been true for them. The more I thought about it, the more that structure reflected the philosophy I’d been talking about on this podcast all along. We don’t have to choose between honoring history and honoring our own experience. We can do both.

It wasn’t until I reached that point that I finally pushed my chair back from the computer and simply stared at the screen for a while. Every feature I’d built over the previous weeks had started talking to every other feature. The altar connected to the journal. The journal connected to the library. The library informed future rituals, which in turn created new journal entries. None of it existed in isolation anymore because that’s not how spiritual practice works in real life. Our experiences shape our understanding, our understanding influences our rituals, and our rituals inevitably change the way we experience the world. I hadn’t consciously designed that interconnectedness. I had simply followed each idea to its natural conclusion until, one evening, I realized I was no longer looking at a podcast website.

I was looking at a place where people could practice.

That realization landed with a weight I wasn’t expecting because, in an instant, the questions I’d been asking for weeks completely changed. Up until then, I’d been focused on features. What should I build next? What functionality was missing? How could I make this page work better? Those questions suddenly felt far less important than the one quietly replacing them. It wasn’t about technology anymore. It wasn’t even about the website. It became a question I found myself returning to over and over again.

What kind of place do I want people to come home to?

And once I asked that question, every decision after it became surprisingly clear.

By the time I reached that realization, everything changed.

I wasn’t asking myself what features belonged on a website anymore. I was asking a much more personal question. If someone arrived here carrying the same fears, limitations, or uncertainties I’d been thinking about for weeks, what would actually help them? Not what would impress them. Not what would make the platform feel modern or polished. What would genuinely make their spiritual practice a little more accessible than it had been the day before?

Once that became the question, the answers almost started revealing themselves.

The first barrier I kept coming back to was the simplest one of all. What happens when someone doesn’t have a place to practice? It sounds like such an obvious question now, but at the time I couldn’t stop turning it over in my mind. Spirituality is often presented as something that requires physical space. We see beautiful altars online filled with candles, statues, crystals, herbs, and carefully curated ritual tools, and while I genuinely love seeing those spaces because they can be deeply inspiring, they can also unintentionally send a message that says, “This is what spiritual practice is supposed to look like.” The truth is, for many people, it doesn’t look anything like that.

I kept thinking about the person whose entire practice has to remain invisible. Maybe they still live with parents who wouldn’t understand. Maybe they share an apartment with roommates. Maybe they’re navigating circumstances where displaying an altar would create conflict they simply aren’t in a position to face. Others might be living with disabilities or chronic illnesses that make setting up and maintaining a physical ritual space far more difficult than most of us realize. Some people simply don’t have the financial freedom to slowly collect the tools they see everywhere online. None of those realities make someone less devoted, less sincere, or less worthy of building a relationship with the divine. They simply mean their circumstances look different.

That’s why I want to be very clear about something before I go any further. I am not trying to replace physical practice with technology. I don’t believe a digital altar is somehow better than a physical one, and I certainly don’t think a website can replace the experience of holding herbs in your hands, lighting a real candle, or standing quietly in front of an altar you’ve lovingly built over time. I have a physical altar myself, and I treasure it. If you’re fortunate enough to have that, I hope you continue using it for years to come.

The digital altar was never meant to replace what already exists.

It was meant to exist for the people who don’t have another option.

Once I saw it that way, everything about the idea changed. I wasn’t trying to recreate every physical sensation of ritual inside a browser window because that isn’t possible, and honestly, I don’t think it should be. What I wanted to create was an opportunity. A place where someone could intentionally arrange sacred objects, reflect on their practice, perform devotional work in whatever way felt meaningful to them, and leave knowing that tomorrow, when they came back, it would still be there waiting for them. Not because pixels are inherently sacred, but because intention is.

The more I reflected on that, the more I realized I wasn’t actually designing an altar at all.

I was designing permission.

Permission for someone to stop waiting until life became perfect before allowing themselves to practice. Permission for the college student who only has ten quiet minutes between classes. Permission for the parent whose children finally fell asleep after a long day. Permission for the person recovering from illness who has enough energy to open a laptop but not enough to gather ritual tools. Permission for anyone who has quietly believed that their circumstances somehow disqualified them from meaningful spiritual work.

That idea became incredibly important to me because I don’t believe the divine waits for our lives to become convenient. If anything, I think some of our deepest moments of connection happen in the middle of imperfect circumstances. They happen in hospital rooms, parked cars, tiny apartments, lunch breaks, and sleepless nights. They happen when we bring whatever we have to the relationship instead of mourning what we don’t.

Once the altar began taking shape, another question naturally followed.

What happens after the ritual is over?

Every meaningful practice eventually leaves something behind. Sometimes it’s a profound insight that feels impossible to put into words. Sometimes it’s a dream that lingers in your mind for days, refusing to be forgotten. Sometimes it’s something much quieter, like realizing that a particular herb, crystal, or deity has begun taking on deeply personal meaning in your life that no book could have predicted. Those moments deserve somewhere to live, because over time they become the story of your spiritual journey.

And that’s where the next piece of the puzzle began revealing itself…

By the time everything finally started fitting together, I realized something that still makes me smile every time someone congratulates me on “launching a website.”

Because that’s not what it feels like.

I understand why people call it that. From the outside, that’s exactly what it looks like. You open your browser, type in a web address, and there it is. It’s a website. They’re not wrong. But after everything that went into creating it, after all the late nights, philosophical rabbit holes, coding bugs, moments of frustration, and quiet realizations that shaped what eventually appeared on the screen, calling it a website almost feels like calling a house a pile of lumber. It’s technically accurate, but it completely misses the story of how it came to be.

One thing that surprises almost everyone is how quickly the actual platform came together. The philosophy behind Salt & Sovereignty has been developing ever since I started this podcast. My relationship with Hekate, the conversations we’ve shared here, the questions I’ve wrestled with about healing, sovereignty, accessibility, and spirituality, those have been unfolding for a long time. But the actual building? The coding, the designing, the countless decisions that eventually became this platform? That all happened over the course of just three weeks. Looking back, I’m honestly not sure I would recommend doing it that way, but at the time I don’t think I knew any other option.

Those three weeks are difficult to describe because they never really ended at the computer. I would wake up thinking about a feature I’d been struggling with the night before. I’d drive to work mentally rearranging ideas in my head. Throughout the day I’d catch myself solving little programming problems while answering emails or eating lunch, then I’d rush home, open my laptop, and immediately start building again before I even had time to think about how tired I was. Hours disappeared without me noticing. I’d glance at the clock expecting it to be seven in the evening, only to discover it was almost midnight, and somehow I still wasn’t ready to stop because every time one piece finally clicked into place, I could suddenly see the next piece waiting for me.

I’ve heard people describe experiences like that as obsession, but I don’t think that word quite captures what it felt like. Obsession implies something unhealthy or compulsive, and while I’m sure there were moments where I probably should have stepped away from my computer a little sooner, what I experienced felt different. It was as though every solution revealed something that had been hidden just beneath the surface. I’d solve one problem, and suddenly another idea would make perfect sense. I’d connect one system, and all at once I could see three more ways it could support someone’s practice. I wasn’t forcing ideas into existence. I was uncovering them one by one, almost like assembling a puzzle without ever having seen the picture on the front of the box.

Of course, none of that happened in isolation. As much as this platform has become an expression of my own vision, there is another person whose fingerprints are quietly woven throughout this story, even if they never touched a single line of code.

My Partner.

I don’t think he fully realized what he was agreeing to when I started saying things like, “I just have one more thing I want to finish.” If you’ve ever built something creative, you already know that “one more thing” is almost never just one more thing. One finished feature turns into another idea. That idea uncovers a bug. Fixing the bug creates an opportunity to improve something else, and before you know it, the evening has disappeared. For three weeks, that cycle repeated itself almost every single day.

To say he barely saw me during those weeks wouldn’t be entirely accurate because we still had dinner together, still talked about our days, and still shared those little moments that make up ordinary life. But there’s no denying that this project occupied a huge amount of my attention, and he could have responded to that in any number of understandable ways. He could have become frustrated. He could have asked me to slow down. He could have questioned why I was pouring so much energy into something that, at least from the outside, probably looked like an overly ambitious website.

Instead, he simply believed in me.

He encouraged me when I felt stuck. He celebrated the little victories that probably made absolutely no sense to anyone who isn’t a programmer. He patiently listened while I excitedly explained features that sounded completely ridiculous out of context, and perhaps most importantly, he never made me feel guilty for caring so deeply about something that only existed in my imagination until it slowly became real. We often celebrate the people whose names are attached to creative projects, but I think every meaningful dream is quietly carried by people standing just outside the spotlight. They may never receive public recognition, but without them, many of those dreams would never leave the ground. So if you’re listening, to my partner, thank you. I hope you know how much of this belongs to you too.

Then, because life has a wonderfully ironic sense of timing, launch week arrived.

After three weeks of pouring nearly every spare moment into this project, I got sick.

Not just the kind of tired that comes from working too much, but genuinely sick. My throat hurt, my ears were plugged, I felt exhausted, and for the first time in a long time I had to take an entire day off from my full-time job because I simply couldn’t function. If you know me personally, you know that isn’t something I do lightly. Normally I push through just about everything, but this time my body made the decision for me.

Lying in bed that day was strangely emotional because my mind kept drifting back to the project. Not because I was worried about missing a deadline or because anyone was waiting impatiently for launch day to arrive. The thought that kept returning wasn’t about the website at all. It was about the people I had spent weeks imagining while I built it. I found myself wondering if someone out there might eventually stumble across it during a difficult season of their life. Maybe they’d been curious about witchcraft for years but had never known where to begin. Maybe they’d been practicing quietly, feeling isolated and unsure whether there was room for them in this community. Maybe they’d simply been looking for a place that didn’t immediately tell them they were doing everything wrong.

I wanted that first experience to feel like someone opening the front door of a home.

Not a perfect home.

Not a finished home.

Just one where they felt genuinely welcome.

That’s probably the biggest reason I smile whenever someone says, “Congratulations on launching your website.” Because in my heart, that’s never been what this feels like. A website is something you visit, accomplish a task, and eventually leave behind. That’s never been my hope for Salt & Sovereignty. I don’t want people to feel like they’re opening another app or scrolling another feed. I want them to feel like they’re arriving somewhere intentionally built for them, somewhere they can return to again and again as their own practice continues evolving.

The funny thing is, even now, I don’t think it’s finished.

Actually, I know it isn’t.

There are still rooms that haven’t been built yet. There are ideas sitting in notebooks waiting for the right time, features that haven’t even begun to take shape, and dreams that still feel far too ambitious to say out loud. I’m sure if you come back a year from now, the platform will look different than it does today. Five years from now, I hope it looks different still. Growth isn’t something I’m trying to avoid. In fact, I hope Salt & Sovereignty never reaches a point where it stops growing because spiritual practice itself is never truly finished. It’s something we continue discovering for the rest of our lives.

More than anything, though, I hope one thing never changes.

I hope that whether someone has been practicing for thirty years or they’re nervously typing the word “witchcraft” into a search bar for the very first time, they feel like there’s a place for them here. Whether they follow Hekate, Brigid, Odin, Aphrodite, the spirits of the land where they live, or they aren’t even sure what they believe yet, I hope they feel welcomed instead of judged. The world already has enough places that tell people they don’t belong until they’ve earned it. I have no interest in building another one.

Earlier in this episode, I shared the experience I had during meditation when I heard Hekate say, “You are to be my voice.” For a long time, I assumed those words referred only to this podcast. Maybe they did. But over the past few weeks, I’ve found myself wondering whether I misunderstood what they were really asking of me.

Not because I think I’m meant to become someone’s spiritual authority.

Quite the opposite.

If these conversations, this podcast, and now this platform accomplish anything worthwhile, I hope it’s this. I hope they help people become a little quieter inside, just long enough to hear their own voice. I hope they feel empowered to ask honest questions, to explore thoughtfully, to honor tradition without becoming imprisoned by it, and to trust that their own lived experience has value too. My relationship with the divine isn’t supposed to become your relationship with the divine. It can’t. Your path will be shaped by your own experiences, your own healing, your own questions, and your own encounters with the sacred. That’s exactly how I think it should be.

People have already asked me why so much of this platform is free, and the answer is surprisingly simple.

Accessibility can’t be the mission if accessibility only exists for the people who can afford it.

There will always be ways to support what I’m building if someone chooses to. You can listen to the podcast, share an episode with a friend, join Patreon when it launches, or simply tell someone that Salt & Sovereignty exists. Every single one of those things helps more than you probably realize. But I never wanted the heart of this project to become something locked behind a paywall because the entire reason it exists is to remove barriers, not create new ones.

So I don’t really know what Salt & Sovereignty will become from here.

Maybe it will remain a small corner of the internet.

Maybe one day it will grow into something far beyond anything I can imagine sitting here today.

Honestly, I’m at peace with either outcome.

Because somewhere along the way, success stopped being measured by numbers.

If one person discovers they don’t need perfect circumstances to begin building a meaningful spiritual practice, this project will have been worth it.

If one person living with chronic illness realizes their relationship with the divine is no less valuable because their body has changed, it will have been worth it.

If one person who has spent years believing they were doing everything wrong finds the confidence to trust their own lived experience, then every late night, every frustrating bug, every hour of coding, every moment of doubt, and every one of those three extraordinary weeks will have served a purpose.

Thank you for being here.

Thank you for listening.

And thank you for allowing me to finally tell you the story behind what I thought was just a website.

Until next time…

Honor the truth that keeps you sovereign.
